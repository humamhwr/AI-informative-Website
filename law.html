AI in Law

The discovery process, sifting through documents, in law is often overwhelming for humans. Automating this process is a more efficient use of time. Startups are also building question-and-answer computer assistants that can sift programmed-to-answer questions by examining the taxonomy and ontology associated with a database.

The first time AI was seen in the law industry, it was in the document review side with litigation for discovery and that certainly probably the biggest role that's playing till now. The second place that AI was seen it a very fairly materially is on the due diligence side so it's similar sort of document review. The areas of contract development and document formation and research are the big areas. The new frontiers and we have been seeing a lot of it with companies like Blue Jay and others. Their goal with this research is to make it more efficient by being able to serve obtain answers.

These tools are being developed by a wide variety of tech companies and legal tech companies. Thomson Reuters companies and Blue Jay legal are the two biggest in the industry.

Blue Jay legal are trying to figure out where to use these technologies going forward. They are going to be using a lot of these tools to simulate how courts will make decisions to predict how courts are going to make decisions in the future. Blue Jay legal has developed an artificial intelligence software called tax foresight which helps accountants and lawyers figure out how courts would make decisions in difficult areas of tax law. The user inputs answers to a series of questions then once they hit compute, a result is calculated with the percentage of confidence in the result similar court cases.
